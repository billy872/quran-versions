Historical Context

How Did This Happen?

From oral recitation to multiple printed versions.

610–632 CE
Oral transmission begins. Early Arabic script lacked vowels and consonant dots — the same word could be read multiple ways.[5]
~650 CE
Caliph Uthman's standardization. One consonantal text compiled. Variant codices destroyed. But the ambiguous script still allowed multiple vocal readings.[6]
8th Century
Famous readers emerge — Nafi' (Medina), Aasim (Kufa), Ibn Kathir (Mecca) — each developing distinct recitation traditions from the Uthmanic text.
936 CE
Ibn Mujahid canonizes 7 readings. Two readers who recited differently were tried and forced to recant.[7]
1429 CE
Ibn al-Jazari adds 3 more readings → total of 10 canonical qira'at.[8]
1924
The turning point. Egypt publishes the Cairo Edition using Hafs. Saudi mass distribution follows. Printing technology — not early consensus — created Hafs dominance.[2]

Conclusion

What the Evidence Shows

The claim that "all Qurans are identical" is demonstrably false.

10 canonical qira'at exist with 20 transmissions. Thousands of documented textual variants. Hafs became dominant in the 20th century through printing, not consensus. Early manuscripts confirm script ambiguity.

This is not pronunciation or dialect — these are substantive textual differences recognized within Islamic tradition itself.
