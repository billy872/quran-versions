# Different Types of Qurans: A Comprehensive Research Report

## Executive Summary

The Quran exists in multiple canonical recitations (qira'at), not as a single uniform text. This document presents research on:
- The different kinds of Qurans
- Their historical background
- Which is most popular
- The origin of the most popular version

## Key Finding: 10+ Canonical Versions Exist

**There are at least 10 canonically accepted Quranic recitations (qira'at)**, each with documented textual differences including:
- Different words
- Different verb forms
- Singular vs. plural nouns
- Different pronouns
- Active vs. passive voice
- Vowel variations

---

## 1. THE DIFFERENT KINDS OF QURANS

### A. The Seven Canonical Readings (Qira'at)

The "Seven Readings" were formalized in the 10th century CE by Abu Bakr Ibn Mujahid (died 324 AH/936 CE):

| Reader | Location | Died | Primary Transmitters | Geographic Use Today |
|--------|----------|------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Nafi' al-Madani** | Medina | 169 AH (785 CE) | Warsh, Qalun | North Africa, West Africa |
| **Ibn Kathir al-Makki** | Mecca | 120 AH (738 CE) | Al-Bazzi, Qunbul | Rarely used |
| **Abu 'Amr al-'Ala'** | Basra | 154 AH (770 CE) | Al-Duri, Al-Susi | Parts of Sudan, West Africa |
| **Ibn Amir ad-Dimashqi** | Damascus | 118 AH (736 CE) | Hisham, Ibn Dhakwan | Parts of Yemen |
| **Aasim ibn Abi al-Najud** | Kufa | 127 AH (745 CE) | **Hafs**, Shu'bah | **Most of Muslim world** |
| **Hamzah az-Zaiyyat** | Kufa | 156 AH (772 CE) | Khalaf, Khallad | Rarely used |
| **Al-Kisa'i** | Kufa | 189 AH (804 CE) | Al-Layth, Al-Duri | Rarely used |

### B. Three Additional Canonical Readings

Later scholars (particularly Ibn al-Jazari, died 1429 CE) added three more readings:

| Reader | Location | Died | Transmitters |
|--------|----------|------|--------------|
| **Abu Ja'far** | Medina | 130 AH | 'Isa Ibn Wardan, Ibn Jummaz |
| **Ya'qub al-Yamani** | Basra | 205 AH | Ruways, Rawh |
| **Khalaf al-Bazzar** | Baghdad | 229 AH (844 CE) | Ishaq, Idris |

### C. Total: 10 Canonical Readings with 20 Transmissions

Each reader has two primary transmitters, creating **20 distinct canonical transmission traditions**.

## 2. HISTORICAL BACKGROUND

### Origins in the 7th Century

**Early Transmission Issues:**
- The Quran was initially transmitted orally
- Early Arabic script (Hijazi/Kufic) lacked:
  - Vowel markers (harakat)
  - Consonant dots (i'jam) to distinguish similar letters
  - This ambiguity allowed multiple readings of the same text

**Example:** The basic symbol ب could represent:
- baa' (ب)
- taa' (ت)
- thaa' (ث)
- nuun (ن)
- yaa' (ي)

### Uthman's Standardization (circa 650 CE)

**Problem:** Regional variations were causing disputes among Muslims.

**Caliph Uthman's Solution:**
- Compiled one standardized consonantal text
- Sent copies to major Islamic centers
- Ordered destruction of variant codices

**But:** The ambiguous script still allowed multiple vocal readings

### Canonization Process (10th Century)

**Ibn Mujahid (died 936 CE):**
- Selected 7 readings as canonical
- Based on criteria:
  1. Conformity to Uthmanic consonantal text
  2. Consistency with Arabic grammar
  3. Authentic chain of transmission (isnad)

**Result:** Readings not meeting these criteria were declared "shadh" (irregular/rejected)

## 3. THE MOST POPULAR QURAN: HAFS 'AN 'ASIM

### **Hafs is the dominant reading used by ~95% of Muslims worldwide**

**Full Name:** Hafs ibn Sulayman (died 180 AH/796 CE)

**Chain of Transmission:**
- Reader: Aasim ibn Abi al-Najud (Kufa, died 127 AH/745 CE)
- Transmitter: Hafs ibn Sulayman

### Why Hafs Became Dominant

**Historical Turning Point: 1924**

The **1924 Cairo Edition** (Egyptian standard edition):
- Published by Al-Azhar University
- Adopted the Hafs reading exclusively
- Mass-produced and distributed globally

**Factors in Hafs Dominance:**
1. **Ease of recitation** — considered simpler to learn
2. **Political backing** — Egyptian/Saudi government support
3. **Mass printing** — King Fahd Quran Printing Complex (Saudi Arabia) distributed millions of Hafs Qurans globally
4. **Technological advantage** — printing press made one version ubiquitous

**Scholar Ingrid Mattson:** "Mass-produced printing press mushaf increased availability of the written Quran, but also made one version widespread at the expense of diversity of qira'at."

## 4. THE SECOND MOST USED: WARSH 'AN NAFI'

## 5. DOCUMENTED TEXTUAL DIFFERENCES

### Scale of Variation

**Between Hafs and Warsh alone:**
- **1,354 accepted differences** (documented in Islamic reference works)
- Across all 10 canonical readings: **~3,000+ variants**
- Total variants (including non-canonical): **10,243** (Encyclopedia of Quranic Readings)

### Types of Differences

#### A. Extra Words

**Quran 57:24 (Q57:23 in Warsh)**
- Hafs: "Allah **huwa** l-ghaniyu" (Allah **is** the self-sufficient)
- Warsh: "Allah l-ghaniyu" (Allah, the self-sufficient)

**Quran 3:133**
- Hafs: "**Wa**saari'uu" (**And** hasten)
- Warsh: "Saari'uu" (Hasten)

#### B. Different Letters (Changing Meaning)

**Quran 2:140 (Q2:139 in Warsh)**
- Hafs: "**taquluna**" (you [plural] say)
- Warsh: "**yaquluna**" (they say)

**Quran 21:4**
- Hafs: "**qaala**" (He said)
- Warsh: "**qul**" (Say!)

**Quran 43:19**
- Hafs: "**'ibadu** l-rahmani" (slaves of the Most Gracious)
- Warsh: "**'inda** l-rahmani" (with the Most Gracious)

#### C. Vowel Differences (Changing Grammar/Meaning)

**Quran 1:4 (Q1:3 in Warsh) — Famous Example**
- Hafs: "**Maaliki** yawmi al-din" (Owner of the Day of Judgment)
- Warsh: "**Maliki** yawmi al-din" (King of the Day of Judgment)

**Quran 2:184 (Q2:183 in Warsh) — Legal Implications**
- Hafs: "ta'aamu **miskiinin**" (feeding **a poor man** — singular)
- Warsh: "ta'aami **masakiina**" (feeding **poor men** — plural)
  - *This affects Islamic law on fasting compensation*

**Quran 3:146 — Theological Change**
- Hafs: "**qatala**" (many a prophet **fought** — active voice)
- Warsh: "**qutila**" (many a prophet **was killed** — passive voice)

**Quran 28:48**
- Hafs: "**sihraani**" (two works of **magic**)
- Warsh: "**saahiraani**" (two **magicians**)

#### D. Basmalah Difference

**Major Structural Difference:**
- **Hafs tradition:** Basmalah ("In the name of Allah...") is verse 1 of each surah
- **Warsh tradition:** Basmalah is NOT a verse, but a separator between surahs

**Result:** Hafs Quran has **452 extra words** compared to Warsh (113 surahs × 4 words)

## 7. SCHOLARLY CONSENSUS

### Islamic Traditional View

**Most traditional scholars hold:**
- All 10 canonical qira'at are divinely revealed
- Variations trace back to Prophet Muhammad
- Differences come from "seven ahruf" (seven modes) mentioned in hadith

**Evidence:**
- Sahih al-Bukhari 4992: "This Quran has been revealed to be recited in seven different ways [ahruf]"

### Alternative Islamic View

**Minority of scholars argue:**
- Only ONE original recitation existed
- Variants arose from regional dialects, forgetting, and scribal interpretation
- The 10 readings represent human transmission, not revelation

**Notable proponents:**
- Taha Husayn
- S.V. Mir Ahmed Ali (Shia Ithna-Ashari view)

### Western Academic View

**Consensus among secular scholars:**
- Variations reflect oral transmission complexities
- Early Arabic script ambiguity led to multiple interpretations
- Canonization was a historical process, not divine preservation

## 9. CONCLUSION

### Summary of Findings

**Multiple Qurans Exist:**
- 10 canonically accepted readings (qira'at)
- 20 distinct transmissions
- Thousands of documented textual variants

**Most Popular: Hafs 'an 'Asim**
- Used by ~95% of Muslims
- Became dominant through 1924 Cairo printing
- Origin: Kufa (Iraq), 8th century CE
- Based on reading of Aasim, transmitted by Hafs

**Second Most Popular: Warsh 'an Nafi'**
- Used in North/West Africa (~3-5% of Muslims)
- Origin: Medina, 8th century CE
- Based on reading of Nafi', transmitted by Warsh

**Historical Background:**
- Oral transmission → regional variations
- Uthman's codex (650 CE) → standardized consonantal text
- Ambiguous script → multiple vocal readings
- Ibn Mujahid (936 CE) → canonized 7 readings
- Modern printing → Hafs dominance

### Implications

**For the Claim "All Qurans Are Identical":**
- Demonstrably false
- 1,000s of documented differences exist
- Even traditional Islamic scholarship acknowledges variants

**For Truth Investigation:**
- Multiple textual traditions complicate claims of perfect preservation
- Manuscript evidence shows textual evolution
- Canonization was a human historical process
