# Image Sources for Quran Manuscripts

This document lists where to find high-quality images of different Quranic manuscripts and versions for educational purposes.

## Early Manuscripts (Pre-10th Century)

### 1. Birmingham Quran Manuscript (568-645 CE)
- **Source:** University of Birmingham, Cadbury Research Library
- **URL:** https://www.birmingham.ac.uk/facilities/cadbury/TheBirminghamQuranManuscript.aspx
- **Image Archive:** https://vmr.bham.ac.uk/Collections/Mingana/Islamic_Arabic_1572a/
- **What to see:** Hijazi script, no dots or vowels, oldest radiocarbon-dated Quran
- **License:** Usually educational use permitted

### 2. Sana'a Manuscript (Before 671 CE)
- **Source:** Digital images available from academic publications
- **Description:** Contains palimpsest (erased undertext) with variant readings
- **Key Paper:** Sadeghi & Bergmann (2010) in journal Arabica (includes images)
- **What to see:** Two layers of text, showing textual evolution

### 3. Codex Parisino-petropolitanus (7th Century)
- **Source:** Bibliothèque nationale de France (BnF)
- **URL:** https://gallica.bnf.fr/ (search "Arabe 328")
- **Also:** National Library of Russia
- **What to see:** Early Hijazi script without diacritics

### 4. Topkapi Manuscript (7th-8th Century)
- **Source:** Topkapi Palace Museum, Istanbul
- **Academic:** De Gruyter publications by Rami Hussein Halaseh (2024)
- **What to see:** Kufic script, traditionally attributed to Uthman

### 5. Blue Quran (9th-10th Century)
- **Source:** Metropolitan Museum of Art
- **URL:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection (search "Blue Quran")
- **Also:** Brooklyn Museum
- **What to see:** Indigo-dyed parchment with gold ink, stunning Kufic calligraphy

## Modern Printed Editions

### 6. Hafs Quran (1924 Cairo Edition)
- **Source:** King Fahd Quran Printing Complex publications
- **Available:** Most Islamic bookstores, digital versions online
- **What to see:** Modern standard Arabic with full diacritics

### 7. Warsh Quran (North African Edition)
- **Source:** Moroccan/Algerian print houses
- **Available:** Easy Quran Store (UK), Islamic bookstores
- **What to see:** Maghribi script (Qaaf with one dot, Faa with dot below)

### 8. Comparative Quran (10 Readings)
- **Source:** "Making Easy the Readings of What Has Been Sent Down" by Muhammad Fahd Khaaruun
- **What to see:** Hafs text with marginal notes showing variants from other 9 readings

## Academic Resources with Images

### 9. Corpus Coranicum
- **URL:** https://corpuscoranicum.de/
- **Language:** German/English
- **Content:** High-resolution images of 60+ early manuscript fragments
- **What to see:** Comparative view of different manuscripts

### 10. Islamic Awareness
- **URL:** https://www.islamic-awareness.org/quran/text/mss/
- **Content:** Collection of early manuscript images with analysis
- **What to see:** Codex examples, script evolution

### 11. Wikipedia Commons
- **URL:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/
- **Search terms:** "Quran manuscript", "Kufic", "Hijazi"
- **What to see:** Public domain images of various manuscripts

## Comparison Images (Hafs vs. Warsh)

### 12. Side-by-Side Textual Comparisons
- **Source:** Samuel Green's "The Different Arabic Versions of the Qur'an"
- **URL:** https://answering-islam.org/Green/seven.htm
- **What to see:** Scanned pages showing same verse in both versions with differences highlighted

## Copyright & Usage

- **Academic/Educational Use:** Most institutions allow educational use with attribution
- **Public Domain:** Many early manuscripts are in public domain
- **Always cite:** When using images, always provide source attribution
- **Commercial Use:** Requires permission from copyright holders

## How to Access

1. **University Libraries:** Many have digital collections accessible with library cards
2. **Google Books:** Often has preview pages from academic books
3. **ResearchGate/Academia.edu:** Scholars often share papers with images
4. **Museum Websites:** Most major museums have searchable digital collections

## Image Guidelines for This Project

When adding images to this website:
1. Use public domain or Creative Commons licensed images
2. Always provide source citation
3. Link to original source
4. Specify image date and manuscript origin
5. Include scale/size reference when relevant
